# Porter's Five Forces

Developed by Michael E. Porter at Harvard Business School, **Porter's Five Forces** is the most widely used framework for analyzing industry competitiveness and attractiveness.

## The Five Forces

```
              Threat of
             New Entrants
                  ↑
                  │
Bargaining ←  INDUSTRY  → Bargaining
Power of     RIVALRY       Power of
Suppliers                  Buyers
                  │
                  ↓
              Threat of
             Substitutes
```

## 1. Rivalry Among Existing Competitors

**What to assess:** Number of competitors, industry growth rate, exit barriers, differentiation level, switching costs.

**High rivalry indicators:**
- Many competitors of similar size
- Slow industry growth
- Low switching costs for customers
- High fixed costs or perishable products
- Competitors are diverse in strategies and origins

**Questions to ask:**
- How many direct competitors exist in our space?
- Is the market growing fast enough for everyone?
- How easy is it for customers to switch between us?

## 2. Threat of New Entrants

**What to assess:** Capital requirements, economies of scale, brand loyalty, regulatory barriers, access to distribution.

**High threat indicators:**
- Low capital requirements to enter
- Limited regulatory hurdles
- No strong brand loyalty in market
- Easy access to distribution channels
- No proprietary technology or patents

**Questions to ask:**
- How much would it cost a startup to compete with us?
- Are there regulatory moats protecting our position?
- Could a big tech company easily enter this space?

## 3. Bargaining Power of Suppliers

**What to assess:** Number of suppliers, uniqueness of inputs, switching costs, ability to forward-integrate.

**High supplier power indicators:**
- Few suppliers, many buyers
- No substitutes for supplier's product
- Supplier's product is critical to our business
- High switching costs to change suppliers

## 4. Bargaining Power of Buyers

**What to assess:** Buyer concentration, price sensitivity, switching costs, ability to backward-integrate.

**High buyer power indicators:**
- Few buyers purchase large volumes
- Products are standardized/commoditized
- Low switching costs for buyers
- Buyers can credibly threaten backward integration

## 5. Threat of Substitutes

**What to assess:** Alternative solutions, price-performance trade-off, switching costs, buyer propensity to substitute.

**High threat indicators:**
- Substitute products offer better price-performance
- Low switching costs to substitute
- Buyers are price-sensitive

## Applying the Framework

For each competitor in your landscape, rate each force (1-5) and identify the key drivers:

| Force | Rating | Key Driver |
|-------|--------|------------|
| Rivalry | [1-5] | [What's driving competition intensity] |

| Suppliers | [1-5] | [Most powerful supplier group] |
| Buyers | [1-5] | [Most powerful buyer group] |
